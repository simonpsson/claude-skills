# Narrative Frameworks for Data Analysis

## SCR (Situation, Complication, Resolution)

The most versatile framework for analytical communication.

**Situation:** What is the current state? Establish shared context — the audience should nod along. Keep brief; this is not the point.

**Complication:** What has changed or what is the problem? This is where you introduce tension. The complication is the reason the audience should care.

**Resolution:** What do you recommend, and why? The answer to the complication. This is the primary content.

**Example:**
- Situation: Revenue grew 12% YoY in Q3, consistent with our 10–15% target.
- Complication: However, the growth is entirely driven by price increases. New customer volume declined 8% YoY for the second consecutive quarter.
- Resolution: We need to investigate acquisition funnel conversion, starting with the paid channel where the steepest volume decline appears.

## Pyramid Principle (Answer First)

Lead with the conclusion, then support it with evidence. Opposite of academic writing.

**Structure:**
1. Governing thought (the answer)
2. Key lines (3 supporting arguments)
3. Supporting data (evidence for each key line)

**When to use:** Executive presentations, written memos, any situation where the audience has limited time.

**When not to use:** When you need to build context before the conclusion will land — e.g., when the conclusion is counterintuitive and needs evidence before the audience will accept it.

---

## SPB (Spark, Problem, Bridge)

Used for short narratives and insight summaries.

**Spark:** The hook — a surprising number, a counterintuitive finding, a trend the audience didn't expect.

**Problem:** Why the spark matters. What decision or risk does it create?

**Bridge:** The path forward. What do we do with this?

---

## STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result)

Better suited to retrospective narratives (post-mortems, case studies).

- **Situation:** Context
- **Task:** The objective or challenge
- **Action:** What was done
- **Result:** What happened, measured

---

## Choosing a framework

| Situation | Framework |
|---|---|
| Executive briefing, time-constrained | Pyramid / Answer First |
| Complex problem requiring shared understanding first | SCR |
| Short insight callout (Slack, email, chart annotation) | SPB |
| Post-mortem or retrospective | STAR |
| Multiple findings, equal weight | Horizontal structure (side-by-side key points) |

---

## Common narrative failures

**Burying the lede:** Spending three paragraphs on methodology before stating the finding. The audience disengages before they reach the point.

**Findings without so-what:** "Revenue was $12M in Q2" is a fact, not an insight. Add: compared to what? Is that good or bad? What does it imply?

**Precision without certainty:** Reporting a number to four decimal places when the underlying data has ±5% error implies false confidence.

**Missing the action:** Every analytical narrative should end with a clear next step, a question answered, or a decision enabled. "Interesting, isn't it?" is not a conclusion.
