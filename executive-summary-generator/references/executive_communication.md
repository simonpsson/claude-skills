# Executive Communication Guide

## Understanding your audience

Executives differ from analytical audiences in four ways:

1. **Time constraint:** A C-suite reader spends 60–90 seconds on a document before deciding to read further or stop.
2. **Decision orientation:** They need to know what to do, not how the analysis was done.
3. **Risk sensitivity:** They want to know what could go wrong, not just what is expected.
4. **Context breadth:** They hold many competing priorities simultaneously; your work is one input among many.

Calibrate everything against these four factors.

---

## The 60-second test

Before sending, read only:
- The subject line or title
- The first sentence of each paragraph
- The last paragraph

If a reader who did only this would understand the recommendation and the top risk, the document passes.

---

## Structural rules for executive documents

**Lead with the ask or the finding, never the background.** If the reader needs background to understand the opening, put it in an appendix.

**One recommendation per document.** Multiple recommendations dilute focus and defer the decision. If you have three recommendations, write three documents.

**Quantify the stakes.** "This matters" is not a business case. "This represents $2.4M in at-risk revenue over the next two quarters" is.

**Explicit next step.** Every executive document should end with: "We recommend [action] by [date] by [owner]." No action, no decision.

---

## Language guide

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| "Interesting to note that…" | State the finding directly |
| "It appears that…" / "It seems…" | State what the data shows |
| "We ran a regression analysis…" | "Our analysis shows…" |
| "Statistically significant" | "A real effect, not random variation" |
| "The data suggests we should explore…" | "We recommend [specific action]" |
| "Further investigation is needed" | "The next step is [specific thing] by [owner]" |

---

## Handling uncertainty with executives

Executives expect analysts to have a view, even under uncertainty. "We don't know" is a non-answer unless accompanied by what you would need to know.

**Better approach:**
- State your best estimate ("Our best estimate is X")
- State the confidence level ("We are confident / moderately confident / uncertain because…")
- State what would change the answer ("This assumes Y; if Y is wrong, the answer changes to Z")

---

## Formatting for executive reading

- **Bold the key finding** in the opening paragraph
- **No more than one chart** in a one-page summary
- **Bullet points** over prose for supporting evidence — faster to scan
- **Page 1 is the summary** — everything else is backup
- **Appendix for data:** Move tables, methodology, and detailed breakdowns to an appendix. Don't bury the narrative in a table.

---

## Common failure modes

**Data dump:** A page of tables with no interpretation. Forces the executive to do the analytical work you were hired to do.

**Hedging everything:** Qualifications on every sentence signal uncertainty and reduce credibility. Qualify the things that genuinely matter; don't hedge reflexively.

**Missing the business context:** An analysis that doesn't connect to the business problem being solved is a solution looking for a problem.

**Passive conclusion:** "We hope this analysis is useful" is not a conclusion. End with a decision, a recommendation, or a specific question for the executive.
